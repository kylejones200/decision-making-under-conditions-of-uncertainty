# Decision Making under Conditions of Uncertainty How models, heuristics, and feedback loops help us act when the future
is unclear

::::### Decision Making under Conditions of Uncertainty 

#### How models, heuristics, and feedback loops help us act when the future is unclear
Imagine a world with perfect information where every variable is known,
every future state is observable, every outcome is predictable. In this
world, decision-making becomes arithmetic: plug in the facts and choose
the best option. There are no surprises, no risk, no ambiguity. But this
is not our world.

We live in a world where data is incomplete, events are noisy, and
outcomes hinge on interactions too complex to model fully. Even in
environments awash with data, we face limits on what we can observe,
store, or interpret. Our decisions emerge not from certainty but from
informed approximation. That's the job: to make the best possible choice
in a world that doesn't fully reveal itself.

### Types of Uncertainty
Uncertainty is not monolithic. It comes in layers. One layer is
randomness --- coin flips, weather fluctuations, market
movements --- where the outcomes are inherently probabilistic. This is
aleatory uncertainty, built into the system.

A second type is epistemic uncertainty, which arises not from randomness
but from ignorance. We don't know all the variables. We don't have
complete measurements. We don't fully understand the mechanics. Unlike
randomness, this type of uncertainty can be reduced --- by better
models, deeper domain knowledge, or more data.

Then there's model uncertainty --- a consequence of how we simplify the
world. We use linear regression when relationships aren't linear. We
assume independence where feedback loops exist. Every model, by
definition, is a simplification. Every model leaves something out.

### Why Models Exist
No model can mirror the full complexity of the real world. That's not a
flaw. That's the point. Models are how we compress reality into a form
we can work with. The alternative --- trying to reason directly from raw
data or lived experience --- is untenable for complex decisions.

In finance, we model risk using historical returns. In weather, we model
storm paths using atmospheric data. In logistics, we model demand to
plan routes and staffing. In each case, the model is not the
truth --- it is a stand-in, a device for improving decisions despite
uncertainty.

### Decision-Making Strategies Under Uncertainty
Because we know our models are imperfect, we don't trust them blindly.
We apply strategies that account for what we don't know.

One common method is expected value, which weights outcomes by their
probability. When you can't predict a single outcome, you choose the
option with the best average result.

Another approach is scenario analysis. What if demand drops by half?
What if supply chains collapse? Rather than predicting, you explore
boundaries. This helps prepare for surprises.

Sensitivity analysis asks: how much would this decision change if our
assumptions were slightly wrong? It reveals where decisions are fragile
and where they are robust.

And sometimes, we rely on heuristics. These are shortcuts --- rules of
thumb --- that work well enough when the complexity is too high or the
data too sparse to model precisely. They're not always right, but they
are often good enough.

### The Value of Information
In uncertain environments, better data can improve outcomes. But
gathering data isn't free. More sensors, more surveys, more scraping all
carry costs. The decision to collect more data is itself a decision
under uncertainty.

There's a concept called value of information --- how much better your
decision would be if you had the data. If the gain from better decisions
outweighs the cost of gathering data, it's worth it. If not, act now
with what you have.

Sometimes, more data doesn't help. The system is too random, or the
uncertainty too deep. In those cases, refining the model is more useful
than expanding the dataset.

### Risk Versus Uncertainty
Risk is when you know the odds. Uncertainty is when you don't. A
roulette table is risk. A new market with no history is uncertainty. In
risk, you can optimize. In uncertainty, you hedge. You test. You adapt.
As they Marines say, "Improvise, Adapt, Overcome."

Many business failures come from mistaking uncertainty for
risk --- plugging in assumed probabilities and acting with unjustified
confidence. Wise decision-makers recognize the difference and stay
humble in the face of unknowns.

### Embracing Imperfection
Perfect information is a fantasy. So is the perfect model. Good
decision-makers don't wait for perfection. They work with the best
models available, stress test their assumptions, and act when the moment
demands it.

They also revise. The best decisions are not one-time events. They are
part of feedback loops. You try, you observe the outcome, you refine
your strategy. Decision-making under uncertainty is a process, not a
snapshot.
::::We don't get to choose whether we operate under uncertainty. That's the
default state of the world. What we choose is how we respond to it. We
can freeze up, wait for clarity that never comes, or build models that
help us act in spite of uncertainty.

Good models don't remove uncertainty, but they reduce the wrong kinds of
it. They expose assumptions. They test scenarios. They help us act
sooner and fail less often.

Uncertainty isn't the enemy. In many domains, it's what makes
decision-making valuable. If everything were known, we wouldn't need
judgment. We wouldn't need insight. There would be nothing to decide.
